Scientists Discover New Method for Carbon Capture Using Engineered Microbes

By Dr. Sarah Chen | March 8, 2026

A team of researchers at MIT has developed a groundbreaking approach to carbon capture that uses genetically engineered bacteria to convert atmospheric CO2 into solid calcium carbonate at rates up to 100 times faster than natural processes.

The Breakthrough

The new technique, published today in Nature Biotechnology, modifies the common soil bacterium Bacillus subtilis to express enhanced carbonic anhydrase enzymes. These enzymes dramatically accelerate the natural mineralization of carbon dioxide.

"What we've achieved is essentially speeding up a geological process that normally takes thousands of years and compressing it into days," said lead researcher Dr. James Wilson. "The implications for climate change mitigation are enormous."

How It Works

The process involves three key stages:

  1. Engineered bacteria are cultured in bioreactors with controlled CO2 exposure
  2. The bacteria produce calcium carbonate pellets as a metabolic byproduct
  3. Pellets are harvested and can be used in construction materials or stored permanently

Industry Reaction

The announcement has been met with cautious optimism from the scientific community. Dr. Elena Vasquez of Stanford's Earth Systems Science department called it "potentially transformative" but noted that scaling challenges remain.

  • Pilot plants are planned for Q3 2026 in three locations
  • The research received $45 million in funding from the DOE
  • Patent applications have been filed in 12 countries

Critics, however, warn against viewing any single technology as a silver bullet for climate change.

"We need a portfolio of solutions," argued environmental economist Dr. Robert Park. "Microbial capture is promising, but it doesn't replace the need to reduce emissions at the source."
