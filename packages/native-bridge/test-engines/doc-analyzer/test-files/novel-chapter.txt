Chapter 3: The Letter

The envelope sat on the kitchen table for three days before anyone dared to open it. It was addressed simply to "The Residents" in a hand none of them recognized — thin, spidery letters that tilted slightly to the left, as if written in a hurry or in the dark.

"Someone should open it," Eleanor said on the third morning, setting down her coffee cup with deliberate care.

"Be my guest," replied Thomas from behind his newspaper. He hadn't actually read a word in twenty minutes. His eyes kept drifting to the envelope.

Their daughter, Mira, reached across the table and picked it up. "You're both ridiculous," she said, sliding her finger under the flap.

The paper inside was yellowed, brittle at the edges. A single paragraph, written in the same tilting hand:

"By the time you read this, I will have been gone for exactly one year. The house remembers what you have forgotten. Look beneath the third stone in the garden wall. What you find there will change everything — or nothing at all. The choice, as always, is yours."

There was no signature.

* * *

Thomas folded his newspaper. "Well," he said carefully. "That's certainly... something."

"It's nonsense," Eleanor said firmly. But her hand trembled slightly as she reached for her coffee.

Mira was already pulling on her boots. "I'm going to look," she announced.

"Mira, don't be absurd—"

"Mom." Mira paused at the door, her expression serious beyond her seventeen years. "Someone sent this to us. Someone who knows about the garden wall. Don't you want to know who?"

Eleanor and Thomas exchanged a look — the kind of look that married couples develop over decades, carrying entire conversations in a single glance.

"I'll get my coat," Thomas said quietly.
